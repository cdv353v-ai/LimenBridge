The 167 Hours Problem:
Why Therapy Progress Disappears Between Sessions

By Mark Vdovskikh · Founder, LimenBridge · Physician, 30 years in clinical practice

A patient came in for a session. Chronic back pain, three months of working together, and real, visible progress. At the very end, as she was buttoning her coat, she turned to me and said: "The hardest part isn't the pain anymore. It's Sunday evening before the next appointment. I just don't know what to do with myself."

In that moment, I felt the limitations of our medical system acutely. What could I do right there in the office? Suggest standard self-regulation techniques? Write a prescription? Schedule an extra appointment? These were all partial answers. The real problem lay deeper — in the autopilot of the autonomic nervous system, which, outside the clinic walls, immediately drags the body back into its habitual survival mode.

She left, and I was left thinking about that Sunday evening. About the 167 hours between one weekly session and the next. About what actually happens to the physiology in that large, empty space.

"The clinic is a safe island. But how do you survive the open sea between those islands?"

The answer isn't willpower. You can understand everything intellectually during therapy — the patterns, the triggers, the history. But the nervous system is deeply habitual. If the body is still firing old stress responses on a biological level during the week, you experience a setback. The insights don't disappear, but the felt sense of them does. That's not failure. That's physiology.

I spent years trying to understand what the gap actually does to the nervous system. Why some people cross it intact and others don't. Why the same anxiety that was manageable on Tuesday is unbearable by Sunday.

The answer is simpler than I expected. Between sessions, there is no structure. No predictable cue that tells the nervous system: you are safe now, the work continues. The body, left without that signal, defaults to its baseline — which for most people in therapy is a baseline of chronic low-level alert.

I realized there were hundreds of meditation apps and journaling tools available. But nothing was built specifically around the actual chronobiology of the nervous system — nothing that understood where the body is at 7am versus 10pm, and responded to that difference with precision.

Nothing acted as a structural support system between sessions.

So I decided to build it. That is how LimenBridge came to exist — not as a health-tech startup, but as the engineering solution I needed for that patient on that Sunday evening.

It is not a replacement for therapy. It is a bridge between sessions — designed to help the body hold its ground so the mind can actually heal.

Mark Vdovskikh

Founder, LimenBridge
