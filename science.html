Research & Evidence

Why 28 days? Why morning and evening?
Why silence after the track?

LimenBridge is built on four research areas: habit formation, chronobiology of the autonomic nervous system, auditory modulation of HRV, and between-session support in psychotherapy. Below is what the evidence says — and how it shaped the product.

1. Habit formation: the 28-day structure

The research

In a landmark study at University College London, Phillippa Lally and colleagues tracked 96 people forming new daily habits over 12 weeks. They found that automaticity — the point where a behaviour no longer requires willpower — develops along a curve, not a straight line. The steepest gains happen in the first weeks of consistent practice. The average time to reach stable automaticity was 66 days, but the range was wide: 18 to 254 days. Importantly, missing one day did not derail the process.

What this means for LimenBridge

The 28-day program is designed to capture the period of fastest automaticity growth. The same 14 tracks are repeated intentionally — not because we ran out of material, but because the second listen happens with a nervous system that has already begun to change. Days 29–30 are a structured reflection pause, not a gap.

  • Lally, P., van Jaarsveld, C. H. M., Potts, H. W. W., & Wardle, J. (2009). How are habits formed: Modelling habit formation in the real world. European Journal of Social Psychology, 40(6), 998–1009. DOI: 10.1002/ejsp.674

2. Morning and evening: timing matters for the nervous system

The research

The autonomic nervous system follows a circadian rhythm regulated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus. Cortisol peaks 30–45 minutes after waking (the Cortisol Awakening Response, CAR) and then declines throughout the day. Research shows that morning interventions timed to this window have documented effects on perceived stress and daytime cortisol levels. Conversely, evening is when the body shifts toward parasympathetic dominance — but this transition is often interrupted by accumulated tension and screen exposure.

A 2015 study found that morning exercise enhanced parasympathetic activity during subsequent sleep, while evening exercise activated sympathetic activity. The timing of an intervention changes its effect on autonomic tone.

What this means for LimenBridge

The morning track is designed to work with the CAR — not suppressing activation, but giving it a safe shape. The evening track supports the natural shift toward parasympathetic dominance, helping the body complete a transition that modern life tends to interrupt.

  • Portaluppi, F., et al. (2020). Circadian rhythms of cardiovascular autonomic function. Autonomic Neuroscience, 229, 102689. DOI: 10.1016/j.autneu.2020.102689
  • Yamanaka, Y., et al. (2015). Morning and evening physical exercise differentially regulate the autonomic nervous system during nocturnal sleep in humans. American Journal of Physiology-Regulatory, Integrative and Comparative Physiology, 309(9), R1112–R1121. DOI: 10.1152/ajpregu.00127.2015

3. Auditory modulation of HRV

The research

Heart rate variability (HRV) is a well-established marker of autonomic nervous system regulation. Higher HRV indicates greater adaptability — a nervous system that can shift between activation and recovery as needed. Multiple studies have shown that structured auditory stimuli can modulate HRV.

A 2014 double-blind, placebo-controlled study found that 20 minutes of theta-frequency binaural beats after exercise increased parasympathetic activation (measured by HF HRV) and increased sympathetic withdrawal, compared to placebo carrier tones. The HRV changes during binaural-beat listening predicted 43% of the variance in self-reported relaxation.

A 2025 study with 65 college students confirmed that 20 minutes of theta-frequency binaural beats significantly reduced heart rate, systolic and diastolic blood pressure, and increased parasympathetic HRV markers.

What this means for LimenBridge

The audio tracks are not "relaxing music" in a generic sense. They are structured around frequency ranges and temporal patterns that research has shown to influence autonomic tone. The 10-minute duration is long enough to produce measurable HRV shifts without requiring extended time commitment.

  • McConnell, P. A., et al. (2014). Auditory driving of the autonomic nervous system: Listening to theta-frequency binaural beats post-exercise increases parasympathetic activation and sympathetic withdrawal. Frontiers in Psychology, 5, 1240. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2014.01240 · PMC: PMC4231835
  • Yang, S.-Y., et al. (2025). Effects of binaural beat therapy with different frequencies on autonomic nervous system regulation among college students. BMC Complementary Medicine and Therapies, 25, 220. DOI: 10.1186/s12906-025-04922-x · PubMed: 40483455

4. Between-session support: why the gap matters

The research

Multiple meta-analyses have established that structured between-session activities significantly improve psychotherapy outcomes. Kazantzis et al. (2000, 2010) found a medium effect size (d = 0.53) for therapies that include between-session homework compared to those that do not. The correlation between homework adherence and treatment outcome is r = 0.26 — comparable to the well-established therapeutic alliance-outcome relationship.

However, research also shows that "homework" as a concept carries negative connotations for many clients, evoking evaluation, control, or failure. What works is structured, predictable support that does not feel like an additional task.

What this means for LimenBridge

LimenBridge is not homework. It is not assigned by a therapist and it is not evaluated. It is a self-directed structural support — a predictable cue that tells the nervous system "the work continues" between sessions. The 10-minute duration and the absence of choice (no browsing, no playlists) are designed to minimise the sense of "another task."

  • Kazantzis, N., Deane, F. P., & Ronan, K. R. (2000). Homework assignments in cognitive and behavioral therapy: A meta-analysis. Clinical Psychology: Science and Practice, 7(2), 189–202. DOI: 10.1093/clipsy.7.2.189
  • Kazantzis, N., Whittington, C., & Dattilio, F. (2010). Meta-analysis of homework effects in cognitive and behavioral therapy: A replication and extension. Clinical Psychology: Science and Practice, 17(2), 144–156. DOI: 10.1111/j.1468-2850.2010.01204.x
  • Kazantzis, N., et al. (2016). Quantity and quality of homework compliance: A meta-analysis of relations with outcome in cognitive behavior therapy. Behavior Therapy, 47(5), 755–772. DOI: 10.1016/j.beth.2016.05.002
  • Ryum, T., et al. (2023). Homework as a driver of change in psychotherapy. Journal of Clinical Psychology, 80(3), 489–505. DOI: 10.1002/jclp.23627

5. Why silence after the track

The research

While not directly studied as an isolated variable, the neuroscience of auditory processing supports the value of structured silence after targeted stimulation. The auditory cortex continues processing and integrating stimuli for several minutes after sound cessation. Interrupting this consolidation with new auditory input — notifications, playlist transitions, or interface sounds — disrupts the autonomic shift that the track was designed to produce.

What this means for LimenBridge

After each track, there is no "next up," no autoplay, no interface. Just silence. This is not a missing feature. It is the completion of the intervention.

What LimenBridge is not

Not a clinical tool. It does not diagnose, treat, or substitute for professional care.

Not meditation. There is no technique to learn, no goal to achieve, no progress to compare.

Not a wellness app. There are no streaks, no gamification, no social features.

Not a music library. There is no choice, no browsing, no playlists.

It is structural support for the nervous system — a predictable, time-bound, evidence-informed practice that occupies the space between sessions, between crises, between attempts to feel better.
